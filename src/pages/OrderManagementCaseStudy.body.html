Order Management Concept Filter · Select · Verify · Send

tl;dr

A platform modernization gave us the chance to completely rethink Order Management — and we almost over-designed it. We explored a flashy dashboard concept, let user research kill it, and landed on something that looked deceptively simple: a modernized, refined version of the existing paradigm. The hard part wasn't building something new — it was earning the confidence to know exactly where to push and where to hold back.

Context

A forced migration that was also a design opportunity

A forced tech migration created a rare design opportunity. Order Management is central to the entire trading workflow — it's where every order passes through from entry to execution, and it touches every role along the way: traders drafting orders, compliance reviewing them, operations monitoring fills. Tens of thousands of trades flow through this surface daily at the largest broker-dealers, banks, and advisory firms. A rebuild meant we could rethink the interaction model for one of the most critical tools in the trading workflow.

Where Order Management sits

At the center of every trade.

No matter where an order starts — an on-platform trade ticket, an off-platform integration, a model rebalance, a manual entry — it funnels into this single surface, passes through compliance, then lands as Sent or Unsent. From here it loops back out through monitoring, verification, and post-trade actions before the next order does the same. Every trading workflow eventually meets on this screen, which is why getting it right mattered: a misstep here ripples across every role and every system that touches a trade.

The Legacy Experience

The system users lived in

Here's what users were working with every day — three compounding pain points. Each is worth looking at on its own.

Filters expanded vertically from the top, order details from the bottom — both crushed the orders table. Users had to choose: filter, view details, or see their list. Pick one.

No checkboxes, no visual affordance for selection. Bulk actions required shift-clicking and a right-click context menu. Buttons toggled enabled/disabled with no explanation.

Invisible selection → vague send options → useless verification screen (no individual orders visible, nothing fixable) → no confirmation after sending.

Hypothesis & Research

Where we started

Before jumping into open-ended research, we wanted to start with a point of view. Between what we'd been hearing anecdotally from client incident reports and what we observed in our own initial usability analysis of the legacy app, we had enough to form a hypothesis — and design research around testing it.

Our assumptions

01

Users see order management as an end-to-end workflow — order entry through execution — not just a place to check trade status

02

Fragmentation was the root problem: the workflow had no connective tissue between steps, creating friction at every turn

Our hypothesis

03

A central hub — Bloomberg-style dashboard — would give users a single place to move fluidly through the full journey

Product leaders loved the dashboard direction — it felt sleek, modern, and on-trend for institutional finance. The next step was conducting research to pressure-test our assumptions and see whether this direction would actually hold up with the people using the tool every day.

The research told a different story. Users didn't want a dashboard — they wanted the list, made faster. Below is the full arc: how we tested the hypothesis, where it fell apart, and the work it took to align the team behind shipping the disciplined version.

Designing the research to test our direction

To get a better picture, we designed a two-pronged research plan with ~20 participants across clearing and custody:

1. Exploratory interviews — Define the problem: How do users think about Order Management? What do their workflows look like? What frustrates them?

2. Quick concept feedback: at the tail end of each interview, show two rough lo-fi dashboard concepts to get an early read on our direction. Not a formal concept test — just a gut check.

Overarching Themes

Users see Order Management as a continuous workflow — not a single screen. They need speed and reliability above all else, and will trade flashy features for predictability every time. But they also want control: the ability to tailor their view, configure what's visible, and reduce noise. And because firm types, roles, and experience levels vary widely, the system needs to flex to meet all of them.

"It works like a Honda Accord. Not flashy, but reliable."
— Senior Adviser, Custody firm

This user gave us important additional context: reliability isn't a preference — it's a hard requirement. When a trade is time-sensitive and the system is slow, broken, or inaccessible, that has a direct cost. Downtime isn't an inconvenience; it's a missed execution window.

Pulse checking the dashboard concept

Where the hypothesis broke down

What worked: Users responded to one part of the concept — having order details more readily accessible on the same surface. That was solving a real problem: in the legacy experience, getting to any detail meant navigating away entirely.

Too busy. Users felt overwhelmed — too many panels competing for attention. Account search, alerts, order details, and quick trade were all crammed into one view with no clear hierarchy. The cognitive load was high before they'd even started working.

Wrong things grouped together. Account search and recent accounts felt like account maintenance — users preferred that as a separate surface. Alerts and selected order details were too close together, and it wasn't clear what connected the alerts on the right to the orders on the left.

The list is the thing. The most important element on the screen was what they already had: the list of orders. They just needed to work through it more efficiently — with better color coding for errors and statuses, and order-level details accessible without losing their place.

Simplifying our approach

"Full journey" didn't mean "everything on one screen." The dashboard buried the primary task — working through a large list of orders — in a surface that wasn't designed for it.

The answer was connective tissue, not consolidation — better cross-linking between steps (order entry → order management → execution tracking) so each surface could stay focused on its job.

The Honda Accord test

Every feature decision came back to the same question: does this help fundamental capabilities run more reliably, or does it add another thing that can break? Each addition is a tradeoff against the core experience — a missed must-have is far more costly than a missing nice-to-have.

Product owners had their hearts set on the dashboard — but the excitement was about aesthetics, not user needs. Seeing both layouts mapped side by side made it visually clear how moving everything to one screen added complexity to the core tasks: filtering, working through a list, and zooming into order details.

The reframe

The zooming users needed was between the list and individual order details — check a limit price, review an error, confirm a status — not between the list and full account context.

Design Principles

Rules for every decision.

The research and concept feedback shaped a clear set of guiding principles for the redesign:

01

Protect the table

Nothing shrinks or obscures the order list.

02

Go horizontal

Stop competing for limited vertical space.

03

Make configuration effortless

Ensure setup feels practically automatic.

04

Surface relevant actions

The right next step should feel obvious.

05

One surface, one job

Monitoring and acting — not deep account analysis.

Shipped in V1

  • Horizontal layout
  • Configurable views
  • Visible selection and bulk actions
  • Redesigned send flow
  • Order details panel
  • Active filter chips

Deferred to roadmap

  • Proactive alerting
  • Supporting utilities (live quotes, positions, balances)
  • Deeper cross-linking between workflow steps
Design — Main Surface

A surface built to protect the list

Wanted a filter dropdown and pill-based pattern, but design system team lacked capacity for new components. Pivoted to composing a horizontal collapsible filter panel from existing atoms. Active filter pills above the table show exactly what's included/excluded.

Visible checkboxes, color-coded Buy/Sell badges, row-level warning indicators, configurable columns.

We initially wanted order details to appear in a persistent panel to the right of the grid — keeping the list and detail view side by side. But the design system didn't yet have a component that could house it this way, so for V1 we utilized an expandable drawer that gave users everything in one click while keeping the table visible behind it.

Design — Send Flow

Surfacing the right next step

Checkboxes + "Select..." dropdown with counts (all on page, all matching filters, all buys, all sells, etc.) — intentional, informed choices before sending.

Full transparency — summary stats, orders grouped by category with error/warning badges, individual orders visible with inline error messages, ability to remove problematic orders. Errors must be resolved before proceeding.

After sending, users are automatically taken to view their orders on the next surface — clear, crisp cross-linking that makes it easy to flow through the full order management process from entry to execution to post-execution monitoring, with feedback at every step so users always know where they are and what happened.

Constraints

Working within reality

Every design decision was weighed against four forces: AG Grid's out-of-the-box capabilities, the design system's component library, backend service limitations, and the firm's accessibility standards. The gap between ideal and shippable defined the MVP — and the backlog.

AG Grid's native in-column filters failed accessibility standards and didn't align with existing design system components — so we composed a persistent filter panel from existing atoms instead.

Security logos and live quotes were punted — common stocks and ETFs were feasible, but sourcing was unreliable across the range of mutual funds and fixed income positions, and the performance cost per row was too high.

Account-level data for every trade in a book of business wasn't feasible — the backend service couldn't support the volume, so progressive disclosure of account info was deferred.

Saved views required backend persistence that wasn't in scope — a high-value feature we tracked for post-MVP alongside in-column filtering and other AG Grid capabilities we couldn't fully leverage in V1.

Looking Ahead

Remaining opportunities to tackle

Throughout the design process, we deliberately put a pin in features that didn't make the MVP cut — whether due to design system capacity, backend limitations, or the need to keep the initial release focused. These aren't abandoned ideas; they're the next layer of value, informed by everything we learned shipping V1 and observing the pilot.

Circling back post-MVP

Tap any card to read why it was deferred and what it would unlock.

Visual identifiers for each position in the grid — feasible for common stocks and ETFs, but sourcing was unreliable for more obscure mutual funds and fixed income instruments.

User-configurable presets for filters, columns, and sort order — required backend support for persistence that wasn't available for V1.

AG Grid's native in-column filters didn't meet accessibility standards, and the design system couldn't support a new component. The persistent filter panel was the right V1 call, but a sleeker inline option remains on the table.

Real-time price data inline with orders — punted due to the same data sourcing and performance constraints as security logos.

Contextual access to positions, balances, and other account-level utilities without leaving the order surface — blocked by backend service limitations on pulling account info at scale.

Directional thinking

Where the design tension goes next. Tap any to read more.

The post-pilot pivot partially solved for this with better row-level indicators, but there's more to explore — particularly around enabling users to customize their quick filters with saved views or next-best-action filters that surface what needs attention without requiring manual filtering every time.

Live quotes, account balances, and positions don't need to be on screen at all times — but they need to be one interaction away. A contextual flyout or expandable row that surfaces the right data for the selected order, then collapses back to keep the surface clean.

The system could learn what each user cares about — which filters they apply first, which order types they monitor — and pre-load those views on arrival. "Tell me what I need to know" starts with remembering what I asked for last time.

Quote, positions, and balance data appear across multiple tools in the platform. Building these as shared, modular components means they can be dropped into Order Management contextually — without being rebuilt or taking up permanent real estate.

The design tension

The core question for the next phase: how do you make a surface smarter without making it busier? The answer probably isn't additive — it's about making the existing elements more context-aware, so the same screen serves different needs at different moments without changing shape.

Reflection

What actually shipped.

There's a version of this case study where the punchline is a shiny new dashboard. Instead, what shipped was a better version of what users already trusted — modernized filtering, a layout that respects how people actually scan a list, order details you can reach without losing your place, and a send flow that builds confidence instead of anxiety. The irony is that we explored the bold direction specifically so we could come back to the simple one with conviction. The research didn't just tell us what to build — it gave us the evidence to push back on the flashier path and the discipline to ship only what mattered.
