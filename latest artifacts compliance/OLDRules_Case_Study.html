tl;dr

Across a suite of compliance tools on an enterprise investing platform, we identified where to focus design effort — then redesigned the rule management and creation experience from the ground up. The result: a shipped, user-tested redesign that replaced tribal knowledge with guided workflows and shadow spreadsheets with built-in documentation.

finding the right problem before designing the right solution

This wasn't a project that started with a design brief. It started with two questions: across a complex, sprawling ecosystem of aging compliance tools — where do we even begin? How do we make an impact?

I led the team through a structured discovery and sprint process — analyzing user interviews, synthesizing research, and facilitating design thinking workshops — before a single pixel was touched. The decision about what to design was as deliberate as the design itself.

Step 01 — Discovery

Surveying the Compliance Landscape

Rather than commission new research, we surfaced existing user interviews that had never been acted on. In partnership with product, business, and engineering, we collectively revisited the verbatims and raw feedback in detail — synthesizing themes and affinity mapping together before aligning as a team on the problem space. The goal: build genuine empathy across the team, not just inform a brief.

Step 02 — Design Sprint

Insight → "How Might We"

Making the case for a structured sprint before jumping to solutions

Rather than jumping straight from research to wireframes, our full product team — PM, dev lead, and business analyst — agreed to join me in a design thinking sprint. The goal was collective ownership of both the problem and the direction, not just design sign-off.

We used the synthesized research to generate How Might We prompts, then ideated against them — sketching concepts, discussing technical constraints, and mapping effort vs. impact to decide where to start.

And it worked. This isn't like redesigning a weather app — it's a highly complex, compliance-critical suite built on an antiquated tech stack — used by broker-dealers who feel the consequences of a single misconfigured rule across their entire firm's trades. Having engineering constraints, business logic, and product knowledge in the room while sketching didn't slow us down. It made the ideas exponentially sharper. The range and quality of what we generated together, in one session, would have taken weeks to arrive at through back-and-forth handoffs alone.

Step 03 — Prioritization

Deciding Where to Start

With six HMW areas and a list of concepts, the team worked through effort vs. impact \u2014 talking through business constraints, technical feasibility, and user frequency. The most exciting concept (List Manager) got deferred. The highest-impact, most sequentially logical work got prioritized.

\u2713 Phase 1
Rules List Redesign

Users need to be able to find and understand existing rules before anything else. The list page had good bones but critical gaps \u2014 real search, filtering, and a human-readable rule preview. High impact, manageable scope.

\u2713 Phase 1
Rule Creation Redesign

Getting new rules into the system cleanly \u2014 with guided input, contextual keyword help, and confidence before going live \u2014 was the most consequential workflow. Foundation had to be right before tackling edits.

\u2192 Phase 2
Edit Rule + Change History

Editing and viewing rule change history depends on users having a solid mental model of rules first. Sequencing this after creation and list work made the later problem easier to solve.

\u2192 Deferred
List Manager

The most exciting concept from the sprint \u2014 and the most complex. Sprint edge-case analysis revealed significant downstream complexity (list inheritance, bulk editing, format validation). Deferred, not abandoned.

\u2195\ufe0f

On the Securities Upload question

Research flagged that users modify securities lists more frequently than they create new rules \u2014 raising the question of whether that should be prioritized. We identified a workaround for the most acute pain (market hours uploads), which freed us to focus on the higher-complexity, higher-impact rule creation and list work.

Step 04 — Design

Redesigning the Rules List

The legacy rules list had one job \u2014 display all rules \u2014 and did it without any ability to navigate, filter, or understand. The redesign introduced a three-panel layout that turned the list into a genuine decision-making surface.

01
Three-panel layout: Filter \u2192 List \u2192 Detail

A persistent left filter panel (search, active/inactive, trader type, keyword/value), a center rules list with sortable columns and result count, and a right detail panel showing rule logic in plain English alongside a rule timeline summary. Users no longer need to click into a rule to understand what it does.

02
Plain language rule logic

Rule logic is rendered as human-readable sentences rather than raw conditional strings. "If the Account Number on the order is one of the following values... and the Action is Buy... then the order will be rejected." Compliance officers can field trader calls without hunting through opaque logic.

03
Rule Timeline Summary + Change History link

Each rule shows create date, created by, last modified date, and last modified by \u2014 directly on the detail panel. A "View Rule Change History" link provides the deeper audit trail. This begins to replace the Excel/SharePoint shadow system without requiring a full audit log overhaul in Phase 1.

Redesigning Rule Creation

The legacy creation experience assumed expert knowledge. The redesign built that knowledge into the flow \u2014 replacing a blank form with a guided wizard that surfaces context exactly when users need it.

The core structural shift: from a single overwhelming page to a stepped workflow \u2014 Keywords \u2192 Rule Logic \u2192 Order Placed By \u2192 Rule Outcome \u2192 Review & Confirm \u2014 with a persistent plain-language preview building on the left as users progress.

01
Clause-based keyword selection with preview

The keyword page became a three-column layout: a searchable clause list on the left, a preview panel in the center showing each clause's name, usage examples, value type, and tips, and a "selected clauses" summary on the right. Users can preview a clause before committing to it \u2014 eliminating the trial-and-error loop.

02
Structured value input with inline validation

The single 2,000-value text box was replaced with a tabular input: each value gets its own row, with a real-time valid/invalid indicator. A summary bar shows "Valid Values XX,XXX | Invalid Values (Action Needed)." Users know before saving whether their rule is correctly formed. This evolved across two iterations based on user testing feedback.

03
Live plain-language rule preview throughout

As users complete each step, the left panel builds up a plain-English summary of the rule being created. By the time they reach Review & Confirm, the full rule logic is visible in human-readable form \u2014 giving users confidence their rule will do what they intended before it goes live.

Step 05 — Validate

Lo-fi Prototype Testing

We tested with lo-fi prototypes intentionally \u2014 avoiding the trap of over-investing in visual polish before validating the core concept. Unmoderated user testing on early prototypes let us pressure-test direction while there was still room to change course.

Each workflow step was mapped to a specific research question. The areas of highest uncertainty \u2014 clause selection, value input, and the add list flow \u2014 were the focus.

What worked well
General workflow described as "intuitive, smooth, comfortable \u2014 looks great and a lot easier to use"
Plain language rule preview that builds throughout the flow was a standout \u2014 users specifically called it out
Add Clause step: users liked the search, additional info, and flexibility of quick add vs. preview first
Upload pattern felt familiar and seamless; valid/invalid summary well received
Rule Management screen improved \u2014 filtering helpful, rule preview on the right landed well
Review & Confirm: users loved the rule preview at the end
Iterated on
Entry point for adding values was unclear \u2014 users eventually found it but path wasn't obvious enough
Invalid values error handling confusing \u2014 led to Iteration 2 with table-based input and inline validation
No download template for value upload \u2014 users felt unsure without a format reference
Clause preview felt editable \u2014 users tried to interact with it; needed clearer read-only treatment
Order Placed By step lacked the contextual definitions users loved on Order Outcome \u2014 inconsistency noticed
